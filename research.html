NASA Geomagnetic Research

NASA has been monitoring geomagnetic storms for decades, studying the impact of solar flares and coronal mass ejections on our planet. Here's the latest research from NASA's space weather team.

May 2024 Solar Storms

The May 2024 solar storms, part of Solar Cycle 25, were among the strongest since 1989, peaking on May 11 as a G5-class geomagnetic storm. Originating from solar active region Sun(AR3664), these storms caused widespread auroras visible as far south as Greece. They disrupted Earthâ€™s radiation belts, resulting in significant electron and proton flux changes monitored by satellites like Sentinel-6, Galileo, and EDRS-C.

Satellite Impact

  • GOES-16: Experienced temporary data outages due to geomagnetic interference.
  • Sentinel-6: Monitored changes in radiation belts, including a drop in electron flux and subsequent particle injection.
  • Galileo: GPS signals were disrupted due to ionospheric disturbances, impacting navigation accuracy.
  • EDRS-C: Recorded fluctuations in electron flux, affecting data transmission.
  • PROBA-1 & PROBA-V: Observed radiation belt changes, measuring particle flux disturbances.

Mitigation Measures for Satellite Protection

  • Radiation Hardened Electronics: Satellites are designed with radiation-hardened electronics to withstand bursts of energetic particles.
  • Shutdown Protocols: Some satellites may be temporarily put into safe modes to avoid permanent damage during solar storms.
  • Real-Time Monitoring: Space weather monitoring systems provide real-time data on solar activity, allowing operators to take preemptive measures.
